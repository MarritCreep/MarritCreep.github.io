Hardy–Weinberg Equilibrium: What it actually means

Most textbooks state the equation and move on. Here I work through the five assumptions and why violating any single one is enough to drive evolution.

The Hardy–Weinberg principle is usually introduced as a formula: p² + 2pq + q² = 1. Students memorize it, plug in numbers, move on. But the more useful way to think about it is as a null hypothesis — a description of what a population looks like when nothing evolutionary is happening.

The five assumptions

For a population to be in Hardy–Weinberg equilibrium, five conditions must hold simultaneously:

  1. No mutation — allele frequencies don't change due to new mutations arising
  2. Random mating — individuals pair without preference for any particular genotype
  3. No natural selection — all genotypes survive and reproduce at equal rates
  4. No gene flow — no individuals enter or leave the population carrying different allele frequencies
  5. Infinite population size — no genetic drift from random sampling error

The value of HWE isn't in finding populations that satisfy it. No real population does. The value is in measuring how far real populations deviate from it, and asking why.

Why "infinite population" matters

This is the assumption that trips people up most. The requirement isn't literally infinite — it's that the population is large enough that random sampling of gametes doesn't meaningfully shift allele frequencies between generations. In small populations, even neutral alleles can drift to fixation or extinction purely by chance. This is genetic drift, and it's evolution — just evolution without selection.

A concrete example

Imagine a population with a rare allele at frequency q = 0.01. In a population of 10,000, that's ~100 copies — relatively stable. In a population of 20, that's 0.2 copies — which means some generations it'll be present, some it won't. Drift dominates at small N.

Using HWE as a diagnostic tool

In practice, population geneticists use deviations from HWE to detect:

  • Inbreeding (excess homozygotes → non-random mating)
  • Selection (one genotype is over- or under-represented)
  • Population structure (Wahlund effect — mixing of subpopulations)
  • Genotyping error (in sequencing studies)

My exam notes summary

For the exam: the equation p² + 2pq + q² = 1 describes expected genotype frequencies if the five assumptions hold. Given any allele frequency p, you can derive q = 1 − p and then calculate expected heterozygosity as 2pq. If observed heterozygosity differs significantly, one or more assumptions are violated — that's the starting point for the real biology question.
